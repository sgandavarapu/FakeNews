</div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>President Trump signed a new travel ban Monday that administration officials said they hope will end legal challenges over the matter by imposing a 90-day ban on the issuance of new visas for citizens of six majority-Muslim nations. </p> <p>In addition, the nation\u2019s refu\u00adgee program will be suspended for 120 days, and the United States will not accept more than 50,000 refugees in a year, down from the 110,000 cap set by the Obama administration.</p> <p>The new guidelines mark a dramatic departure from Trump\u2019s original ban, issued in January and immediately met by massive protests and then ordered frozen by the courts. The new ban lays out a far more specific national security basis for the order, blocks the issuance only of new visas, and names just six of the seven countries included in the first executive order, omitting Iraq. </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"http://apps.washingtonpost.com/g/documents/national/read-the-full-text-of-the-revised-executive-order/2361/\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"apps.washingtonpost.com\">Read the full text of the revised executive order</a>]</i> </p> <p>Trump signed the new ban out of public view, according to White House officials. The order will take effect March 16.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-linked\"> <div class=\"photo-wrapper\"> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/trump-compare-travel-ban/\"><img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/06/National-Security/Graphics/compare-travel-ban-promo2300.jpg?uuid=IsHPQgKNEeedFJck1I9WZg\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/06/National-Security/Graphics/compare-travel-ban-promo2300.jpg?uuid=IsHPQgKNEeedFJck1I9WZg\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/06/National-Security/Graphics/compare-travel-ban-promo2300.jpg?uuid=IsHPQgKNEeedFJck1I9WZg\"/></a> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/trump-compare-travel-ban/\"> <span class=\"overlay\"> <span class=\"headline\">What Trump changed in the new travel ban </span><span class=\"view-graphic\">View Graphic <span class=\"fa fa-arrow-circle-right\"></span></span> </span> </a> </div> <span class=\"pb-caption\">What Trump changed in the new travel ban</span> </div> <p>\u201cThis executive order responsibly provides a needed pause so we can carefully review how we scrutinize people coming here from these countries of concern,\u201d Attorney General Jeff Sessions said in announcing that the order had been signed. </p> <p>The order also details specific sets of people who would be able to apply for case-by-case waivers to the order, including those previously admitted to the United States for \u201ca continuous period of work, study, or other long-term activity\u201d; those with \u201csignificant business or professional obligations\u201d; and those seeking to visit or live with family.</p> <p>Trump\u2019s campaign, meanwhile, sent out an email asking people to sign a petition in support of the new order.</p> <p>\u201cAs your President, I made a solemn promise to keep America safe,\u201d the email signed by Trump said. \u201cAnd I will NEVER stop fighting until we implement the policies you \u2014 and millions of Americans like you \u2014 voted for.\u201d</p> <p>Democrats and civil liberties groups said Monday that the new order was legally tainted in the same way as the first one: It was a thinly disguised Muslim ban. Trump, in his email, used the phrase \u201cradical Islamic terrorism\u201d to describe his concern with the countries whose citizens would be blocked from acquiring visas. </p> <p>That seems to portend more litigation \u2014 though how soon remains unclear. The attorney general of Washington state, Bob Ferguson, who successfully sued to have the first ban blocked, said he was still reviewing what to do. </p> <p>The new order, Ferguson said, represented a \u201csignificant victory\u201d for Washington state because the administration had \u201ccapitulated on numerous key provisions that we contested in court.\u201d But he said state lawyers would need two or three days to see what action they would take in the court case. </p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"162986\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"What's in Trump's new travel ban\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58bd90a7e4b059a9e423352f\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-06-2017/t_1488820426906_name_Trump_80908_33a36.jpg\" data-uuid=\"6902b022-028b-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"VpCjt3D3FIE\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Jenny Starrs,Danielle Kunitz/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re reviewing it carefully, and still have concerns with the new order,\u201d Ferguson said. </p> <p>The Justice Department argued in a court filing that even if the litigation were to move forward, it should do so at a slower pace, and with the new ban in place. The government noted that visa applicants typically have to wait months and asserted there was \u201cno imminent harm\u201d from the president\u2019s temporary suspension of the issuance of new visas to certain people. </p> <p>That assertion, though, did little to assuage the concerns of Democrats and civil liberties groups, who said the new ban was just like the old. </p> <p>Karen Tumlin, the legal director of the National Immigration Law Center, predicted that federal judges who ordered a restraining order on the earlier ban are likely to do so again, and that pending lawsuits filed by her organization and others will not need to be filed anew. \u201cFrom our vantage point, that litigation lives on,\u201d she said. </p> <p>New York Attorney General Eric T. Schneiderman (D), who joined the legal fight against the first ban, said, \u201cWhile the White House may have made changes to the ban, the intent to discriminate against Muslims remains clear.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">Omar Jadwat, director of the American Civil Liberties Union\u2019s Immigrants\u2019 Rights Project, said, \u201cThe only way to actually fix the Muslim ban is not to have a Muslim ban. Instead, President Trump has recommitted himself to religious discrimination, and he can expect continued disapproval from both the courts and the people.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">The revised travel ban also came under quick fire from refugee advocates, who said it unfairly penalizes refugees without improving U.S. security.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">\u201cPresident Trump still seems to believe you can determine who\u2019s a terrorist by knowing which country a man, woman or child is from,\u201d said Grace Meng, an immigration researcher with Human Rights Watch. \u201cPutting this executive order into effect will only create a false sense of security that genuine steps are being taken to protect Americans from attack, while undermining the standing of the U.S. as a refuge for those at greatest risk.\u201d</p> <p>Officials from the State, Homeland Security and Justice departments defended the new order as a necessary measure to improve public safety. They said the countries named \u2014 Iran, Sudan, Somalia, Libya, Syria and Yemen \u2014 were either state sponsors of terrorism, or their territories were so compromised that they were effectively havens for terrorist groups. Iraq was omitted, Secretary of State Rex Tillerson said, because it is an \u201cimportant ally in the fight to defeat ISIS\u201d \u2014 the Islamic State militant organization \u2014 and Iraq\u2019s leaders had agreed to implement new, unspecified security measures. </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/middle_east/iraq-excluded-from-travel-ban-praises-new-white-house-executive-order/2017/03/06/d3b44d04-027c-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666_story.html?utm_term=.541561572ef2\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Iraq, excluded from travel ban, praises new White House executive order</a>]</i> </p> <p> </p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">The ban is among several measures the administration has introduced in the name of border security. Also Monday, Homeland Security Secretary John F. Kelly said he was \u201cconsidering\u201d separating undocumented immigrant parents from their children to deter people from trying to enter the country. Those people, he said, are often moved to the United States by a \u201cterribly dangerous network\u201d that originates in Central America.</p> <p>Civil rights advocates said Monday that the new ban\u2019s sudden exclusion of Iraq, as well as the omission of other countries with active terrorist groups \u2014 such as Colombia, Venezuela, Pakistan and the Philippines \u2014 underscored the ban\u2019s arbitrariness as a national security measure.</p> <p>The new order provides other exceptions not contained explicitly in previous versions: for travelers from those countries who are legal permanent residents of the United States, dual nationals who use a passport from another country, and those who have been granted asylum or refu\u00adgee status. It removes an exception to the refu\u00adgee ban for members of religious minority groups \u2014 which critics had pointed to as evidence the first ban was meant to discriminate against Muslims \u2014 and it no longer imposes an indefinite prohibition on travelers from Syria. </p> <p>Anyone who holds a visa now should be able to get into the country without any problems, although those whose visas expire will have to reapply, officials said. </p> <p>The order claims that since 2001, hundreds of people born abroad have been convicted of terrorism-related crimes in the United States. It cites two specific examples: Two Iraqi nationals who came to the United States as refugees in 2009, it says, were convicted of terrorism-related offenses, and in October 2014, a Somali native brought to the country as a child refu\u00adgee was sentenced to 30 years in prison for plotting to detonate a bomb at a Christmas tree-lighting ceremony in Oregon. That man became a naturalized U.S. citizen. </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-nation/wp/2017/03/06/trumps-new-travel-ban-still-wont-keep-out-anyone-from-countries-responsible-for-deadly-terror-attacks-in-the-u-s/?utm_term=.8620275fe885\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Trump\u2019s new travel ban still wouldn\u2019t have kept out anyone behind deadly U.S. terrorist attacks</a>]</i> </p> <p>\u201cWe cannot risk the prospect of malevolent actors using our immigration system to take American lives,\u201d Homeland Security Secretary John F. Kelly said.<b></b> </p> <p>The new ban also says that more than 300 people who entered the country as refugees were the subject of active counterterrorism investigations. U.S. officials declined to specify the countries of origin of those being investigated, their immigration status, or whether they had been charged with crimes.</p> <p id=\"U1200289551660tZE\">Charles Kurzman, a sociology professor who studies violent \u00adextremism at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, said that since the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, there have been no fatalities caused by Muslim extremists with family backgrounds in the six countries covered by the new ban. A Department of Homeland Security report assessing the terrorist threat posed by people from the seven countries covered by the president\u2019s original travel ban had cast doubt on the necessity of the executive order, concluding that citizenship was an \u201cunreliable\u201d threat indicator and that people from the affected countries had rarely been implicated in U.S.-based terrorism. </p> <p>The Department of Homeland Security official, speaking on the condition of anonymity, criticized the report as being incomplete and not vetted with other agencies, and he said the administration should not be pressed by the judiciary to unveil sensitive national security details to justify the ban. </p> <p>The order represents an attempt by the Trump administration to tighten security requirements for travelers from nations that officials said represent a terrorism threat. A more sweeping attempt in January provoked mass protests across the country as travelers en route to the United States were detained at airports after the surprise order was announced. The State Department had provisionally revoked tens of thousands of visas all at once. </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/apps/g/page/politics/fact-sheet-and-qa-on-new-travel-executive-order/2174/\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Read the fact sheet and Q&amp;A on the new travel executive order</a>]</i> </p> <p>Officials sought to dismiss the idea that there would be any confusion surrounding the implementation of the new order. They said they delayed implementation so the government could go through the appropriate legal processes and ensure that no government employee would face \u201clegal jeopardy\u201d for enforcing the order. </p> <p>The revisions to the order will make it more defensible in court \u2014 limiting the number of people with standing to sue \u2014 but the changes might not allay all the concerns raised by judges across the country.
