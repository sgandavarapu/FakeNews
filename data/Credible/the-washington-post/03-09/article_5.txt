in climate change?\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58c1cd24e4b059a9e42469c9\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-uuid=\"d2b9d6a2-0511-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"yYe3T3xuXhI\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">President Trump and many of his top aides have expressed skepticism about climate change, while others say human activity is to blame for global warming. So what's the administration's real position? (Peter Stevenson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p><em>This story has been updated.</em></p> <p>Scott Pruitt, the nation\u2019s top environmental official, strongly rejected the established science of climate change on Thursday,\u00a0outraging scientists, environmentalists, and even his immediate predecessor at the Environmental Protection Agency.</p> <p>\u201cI think that measuring with precision human activity on the climate is something very challenging to do and there\u2019s tremendous disagreement about the degree of impact, so no, I would not agree that it\u2019s a primary contributor to the global warming that we see,\u201d Pruitt, the newly installed EPA administrator, said on the CNBC program \u201cSquawk Box.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cBut we don\u2019t know that yet,\u201d he continued. \u201cWe need to continue the debate and continue the review and the analysis.\u201d</p> <p>His comments represented a startling statement for an official so high in the U.S. government,\u00a0putting him at odds not only with other countries\u00a0around the globe\u00a0but also with the official scientific findings of the agency he now leads. President Trump in the past has called the notion of human-fueled climate change a hoax. And other cabinet members,\u00a0including Secretary of Energy Rick Perry, have\u00a0previously questioned the scientific basis for combating global warming.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/03/09/this-climate-lawsuit-could-change-everything-no-wonder-the-trump-administration-doesnt-want-it-going-to-trial/\">This climate lawsuit could change everything. No wonder the Trump administration doesn\u2019t want it going to trial</a>] </i> </p> <p>But Pruitt\u2019s attempt to sow scientific doubt where little exists alarmed environmental advocates, scientists and former EPA officials, who fear he plans to use such views to attack Obama-era regulations aimed at reining in pollution from the burning of coal and other fossil fuels.</p> <p>\u201cThe world of science is about empirical evidence, not beliefs,\u201d Gina McCarthy, the EPA\u2019s most recent administrator, said in a statement. \u201cWhen it comes to climate change, the evidence is robust and overwhelmingly clear that the cost of inaction is unacceptably high. Preventing the greatest consequences of climate change is imperative to the health and well-being of all of us who call Earth home.\u201d</p> <p>She added, \u201cI cannot imagine what additional information the Administrator might want from scientists for him to understand that.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/03/10/proposed-noaa-cuts-would-disarm-our-coasts-in-the-face-of-rising-seas-scientists-say/\">Proposed NOAA cuts would disarm our coasts in the face of rising seas, scientists say</a>] </i> </p> <p>Pruitt\u2019s climate change comments resulted in instant headlines on Thursday. As criticism mounted, White House press secretary Sean Spicer batted back a question about Pruitt\u2019s comments from a reporter who cited Pruitt\u2019s words and how they contradict the scientific consensus on climate change.</p> <p>\u201cThat\u2019s a snippet of what Administrator <span class=\"bckGrdHiLite\">Pruitt</span> said,\u201d said Spicer. \u201cHe went on and said I don\u2019t think we know conclusively, this is what we know. I would suggest that you touch base with the EPA on that. But he had a very lengthy response and that is just one snippet of what the Administrator said.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"32810\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Spicer downplays EPA chief's carbon dioxide emissions denial\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58c1a77ee4b01a7d50409009\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-09-2017/t_1489086521913_name_2017_03_09T190105Z_811479680_RC13B52C8200_RTRMADP_3_USA_TRUMP.jpg\" data-uuid=\"60668f52-04fb-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"Id5a7e8D6co\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">White House press secretary Sean Spicer downplayed EPA head Scott Pruitt's comments on March 9 that carbon dioxide isn't a primary contributor to global warming. (The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>But Pruitt, who\u00a0was visiting the energy industry conference CERAWeek in Houston, also waded into related controversial topics during his CNBC interview. In particular,\u00a0he questioned whether it was EPA\u2019s role to regulate carbon dioxide emissions \u2014 something undertaken through the agency\u2019s Clean Power Plan, the Obama administration\u2019s most significant policy to combat climate change \u2014 and challenged the Paris agreement on climate change.</p> <p>\u201cNowhere in the equation has Congress spoken,\u201d said Pruitt on whether his\u00a0agency is obligated to regulate carbon dioxide. \u201cThe legislative branch has not addressed this issue at all. It\u2019s a very fundamental question to say, \u2018Are the tools in the toolbox available to the EPA to address this issue of CO2, as the court had recognized in 2007, with it being a pollutant?\u2019\u201d</p> <p>(Pruitt was apparently referring to the 2007 Supreme Court decision in\u00a0<em><a href=\"https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/06pdf/05-1120.pdf\">Massachusetts v. EPA</a>,</em>\u00a0in which the court ruled that \u201charms associated with climate change are serious and well recognized<i>\u201d\u00a0</i>and that the EPA had been \u201carbitrary and capricious\u201d in failing to issue a determination on whether greenhouse gases endanger the health and welfare of the public.)</p> <p>The remarks appeared to fundamentally call into question whether the EPA has a role in the regulation of greenhouse gases that drive global warming, including not only carbon dioxide but methane. Last week, Pruitt\u2019s agency withdrew an agency request to oil and gas companies to report on their equipment and its methane emissions, which could have laid the groundwork for tighter regulations.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"wirereuters\" data-duration=\"199083\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Pruitt talks about the future of the EPA at CPAC\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58b1e9fee4b0c99943799203\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/02-25-2017/t_1488054772516_name_20170225_CPAC_Pruitt.png\" data-uuid=\"9e51ab3c-fb99-11e6-aa1e-5f735ee31334\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"gSMvUCP0hR8\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Scott Pruitt addressed the Conservative Political Action Conference on Feb. 25 in Oxon Hill, Md. (The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>Pruitt also dismissed the international Paris climate agreement, which the Obama administration helped\u00a0to lead and which was joined by nearly 200 countries in late 2015, as a \u201cbad deal\u201d for the United States.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s one thing to be talking about CO2 internationally,\u201d Pruitt said. \u201cBut when you front-load your costs, as we endeavored to do in that agreement, and then China and India back-loaded their costs for 2030 and beyond, that\u2019s not good for America. That\u2019s not an America first type of approach.\u201d</p> <p>On the science of climate change, Pruitt\u2019s statements fly in the face of an\u00a0<a href=\"https://www.ipcc.ch/pdf/assessment-report/ar5/wg1/WGIAR5_SPM_brochure_en.pdf\">international scientific consensus</a>, which has concluded that it is \u201cextremely likely that human influence has been the dominant cause of the observed warming since the mid-20th century.\u201d For that matter, they also contradict the very website of the agency that Pruitt heads.</p> <p>The <a href=\"https://www.epa.gov/climate-change-science/causes-climate-change\">EPA\u2019s \u201cClimate Change\u201d website</a> states the following:</p> <blockquote class=\"citation\"><p>Recent climate changes, however, cannot be explained by natural causes alone. Research indicates that natural causes do not explain most observed warming, especially warming since the mid-20<sup>th</sup>century. Rather, it is extremely likely that human activities have been the dominant cause of that warming.</p></blockquote> <p>For this conclusion, the EPA cites the United Nations\u2019 Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, the leading global scientific consensus body that assesses the state of the science roughly every five years.</p> <p>Pruitt spoke with CNBC amidst growing anticipation that the Trump administration will soon move to begin a formal rollback of President Obama\u2019s Clean Power Plan, an EPA policy capping emissions from electricity generating stations, such as coal-fired power plants.</p> <p>Pruitt himself sued the EPA over the Clean Power Plan in his previous role as the attorney general of Oklahoma.</p> <p>And that\u2019s just one of multiple lawsuits that he filed against the EPA \u2013 others were over mercury and air pollution, the agency\u2019s attempts to regulate pollution of waterways, and methane emissions from oil and gas facilities, to name a few.</p> <p>The EPA chief has made several statements in the past that are similar to the present one, perhaps, but not so strongly worded.</p> <p>For instance, <a href=\"http://www.nationalreview.com/article/435470/climate-change-attorneys-general\">writing </a>for National Review in 2016, he stated that \u201cScientists continue to disagree about the degree and extent of global warming and its connection to the actions of mankind.\u201d In his Senate confirmation hearing, meanwhile,\u00a0he stated in a <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/01/18/bernie-sanders-to-scott-pruitt-why-is-the-climate-changing/\">tense exchange</a> with Senator Bernie Sanders that \u201cthe climate is changing, and human activity contributes to that in some manner<em>.\u201d\u00a0</em></p> <p>Another of Pruitt\u2019s predecessors \u2014 now in the business community \u2014 also commented on the science of climate change in the context of his remarks.</p> <p>\u201cThe time for debate on climate change has passed,\u201d Lisa Jackson, President Obama\u2019s first EPA administrator and now\u00a0vice president of\u00a0Environment, Policy and Social Initiatives at Apple, told the Post.</p> <p>\u201cCertainty is what business needs,\u201d said Jackson. \u201cAnd relying on science is something that we do every single day. So now if we\u2019re going to question science, I think it has an impact on more than just some federal rules, or some law, it has a huge impact on human health, the environment, and our economy.\u201d</p> <p><strong>More from Energy and Environment:</strong></p> <p><a
