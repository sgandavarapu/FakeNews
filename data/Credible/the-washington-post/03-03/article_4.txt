It didn\u2019t happen, but more promises have returned. \u201cFoxconn is going to spend a tremendous amount of money on building a massive plant,\u201d Trump said, \u201cand probably more than one.\u201d (Mark Makela/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p> <span class=\"dateline\">HARRISBURG, Pa. \u2014</span> For some residents of this small city, there was something familiar about Foxconn\u2019s recently announced plan to hire up to 50,000 U.S. workers, one of the many hiring pledges from companies rounded up by President Trump in the first weeks of his administration. </p> <p>The only difference was the scale.</p> <p>In 2013, Foxconn\u2019s chairman sent a jolt through this state capital when he said his company \u2014 best known for making Apple iPhones in China \u2014 would invest $30\u00a0million and hire 500 workers for a new high-tech factory in central Pennsylvania.</p> <p>Locals were giddy. Foxconn had a small office here, but this seemed like the start of an entire new industry. Pennsylvania\u2019s governor boasted about the deal. The Brookings Institution think tank hailed Foxconn\u2019s decision as a sign of U.S. manufacturing\u2019s strength.</p> <p>But the factory was never built. The jobs never came. \u201cIt just seemed to fade to black\u201d after the announcement, recalled a local official. It was the start of a mystery, created by a chief executive known to promise projects all over the world that never quite pan out. Yet few people seem to notice. Foxconn and others continue to get credit for deals that never take place. In December, Pennsylvania\u2019s economic development staff was still touting the $30\u00a0million factory that never was.</p> <p>What happened in Harrisburg provides a skeptical lens for viewing the waves of corporate investment promises being used by Trump as evidence that he is following through on his campaign pledge to reinvigorate U.S. manufacturing. \u201cI\u2019m delivering on everything we promised,\u201d Trump said last month during a meeting with manufacturing executives at the White House, noting how firms have announced new hiring and factories. \u201cPeople are saying they\u2019ve never seen so much happen in 30 days of a presidency.\u201d</p> <p>Foxconn, for example, announced shortly after Trump\u2019s election that it plans to invest $7\u00a0billion and hire up to 50,000 workers in the United States. Such a hiring spree would catapult Foxconn from a couple thousand U.S. workers to a major employer on par with Chrysler.</p> <p>Other companies, too, including SoftBank, Alibaba, Intel and IBM, have made an unusual display of loudly announcing their intentions to invest in the United States \u2014 all with price tags in the billions of dollars and the promise of tens of thousands of jobs \u2014 much to Trump\u2019s delight.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela34.JPG?uuid=fDvDiup3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela34.JPG?uuid=fDvDiup3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela34.JPG?uuid=fDvDiup3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela34.JPG?uuid=fDvDiup3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">David Black, head of the Harrisburg Regional Chamber of Commerce said FoxConn\u2019s earlier promise to build a plant created \u201cintense buzz\u201d in the town. (Mark Makela/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>\u201cFoxconn is going to spend a tremendous amount of money on building a massive plant,\u201d Trump said the day after the investment was revealed, \u201cand probably more than one.\u201d</p> <p>But, as Harrisburg learned, the gulf between these eye-popping announcements and what takes place on the ground can be huge \u2014 and frequently overlooked.</p> <p>\u201cThey are scrambling to look good,\u201d Mark Selden, senior research associate at Cornell University\u2019s East Asian program, said. \u201cIt\u2019s a sign of the pressure that Trump is placing on everyone in the world to deliver on his agenda.\u201d</p> <p>Companies tend to view these announcements as an opening bid in a negotiation, said Alberto Moel, a senior analyst with Bernstein Research in Hong Kong. Firms know politicians like the splashy headlines. And if everything falls into place \u2014 the business climate improves, they extract enough concessions such as tax breaks and free land \u2014 they might be able to make it happen.</p> <p>\u201cIt actually puts the onus on the U.S. in a way,\u201d Moel said. \u201cThey are saying: \u2018I\u2019m along for the ride. Here\u2019s my proposal.\u2019\u2009\u201d</p> <p>If the deal falls apart, as it often does, no one is going to talk about it.</p> <p>\u201cThe government themselves aren\u2019t going to remind you of that, Trump has no intention of helping you remember that,\u201d Moel said. \u201cWhat\u2019s the downside?\u201d</p> <p>Trump is far from alone in using corporate announcements as political backdrops. It\u2019s why ribbon-cuttings and ceremonial shovels exist.</p> <p>In 2012, President Barack Obama visited a new $5\u00a0billion Intel computer chip plant in Chandler, Ariz., citing it as \u201can example of an America that is within our reach.\u201d But the facility, known as Fab\u00a042, was never completed. Last week, Intel chief executive Brian Krzanich stood in the Oval Office with Trump to announce a $7\u00a0billion investment \u2014 essentially, Fab\u00a042 was back on track, with a new price tag and a new president. Trump <a href=\"https://twitter.com/realDonaldTrump/status/829410107406614534\" shape=\"rect\" target=\"_blank\">tweeted</a> his praise.</p> <p></p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"wirereuters\" data-duration=\"111480\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Trump announces $7 billion Intel investment in Arizona factory\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"589b6e2ee4b0ed4d25d87ca4\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/02-09-2017/t_1486610082486_name_trumpintel_0208.jpg\" data-uuid=\"e48a54d8-ee32-11e6-a100-fdaaf400369a\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"JcDL-OQVuNk\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">(Video: Reuters / Photo: AP)</span> </div> </div> <p>Even before he was sworn in, it was clear Trump placed greater importance on these job announcements than past administrations.</p> <p>Among the first signs came in December when Tokyo-based SoftBank\u2019s chief executive, Masayoshi Son, stood with the president-elect to announce a plan to invest $50\u00a0billion and create 50,000 new U.S. jobs.</p> <p>Two days after Trump\u2019s inauguration, Foxconn chief executive Terry Gou told reporters in Taiwan that his company planned to spend $7\u00a0billion on a U.S. factory to make computer displays. It wasn\u2019t iPhones \u2014 which Trump has said he wants to be \u201cMade in the USA\u201d \u2014 but Gou said the factory could create 30,000 to 50,000 U.S. jobs. Details were few, but one clue seized on was the presence of a Pennsylvania state trade representative at Gou\u2019s talk.</p> <p>Foxconn said in a statement that it is \u201cexploring a potential investment that would represent an expansion of our current U.S. operations.\u201d But the company could not confirm \u201cthe number of jobs that might be created.\u201d</p> <p>Foxconn is a coveted hardware manufacturer because of its size. Last year, it reported $136\u00a0billion in revenue. It has more than 1\u00a0million employees, mostly in China. A single factory making iPhones or LCD screens can employ tens of thousands of workers. When Foxconn says it will invest in a new plant, the decision can transform a region. That\u2019s why countries all over the world are eager to lure Foxconn to their shores.</p> <p>Gou, Foxconn\u2019s founder, for years has described the United States as a \u201cmust-go market.\u201d</p> <p>In 2014, as the Pennsylvania deal was quietly dying, Gou teased the opening of a LCD display factory in the states. His company talked with officials in Colorado and Arizona. Nothing happened.</p> <p>That same year, the company signed a letter of intent to invest up to $1\u00a0billion in Indonesia. That investment still has not occurred.</p> <p>Foxconn said it would invest $5\u00a0billion over five years in India as part of an ambitious expansion in 2014. The deal would create up to 50,000 jobs, Gou said. But Foxconn\u2019s investment in India has amounted to only a small fraction of what it originally promised.</p> <p>Similar results were seen in Vietnam, where Foxconn committed to a $5\u00a0billion investment in 2007, and in Brazil, where Foxconn spoke of a $10\u00a0billion plan in 2011. The company made its first major foray in Vietnam only last year. In Brazil, Foxconn has an iPhone factory, but its investment has fallen far short of expectations.</p> <p>\u201cThere\u2019s a pattern here,\u201d said Moel, the analyst.</p> <p>Gou\u2019s plans for Harrisburg were revealed in 2013 while he was in the United States on a tour with other Taiwanese business officials to, in part, drum up support for Taiwan\u2019s bid to join the Trans-Pacific Partnership, known as TPP. (Coincidentally, Trump pulled the United States out of the trade treaty in his first days in office.)</p> <p>It was a surprise when Gou said Foxconn planned to invest $30\u00a0million over two years in a factory to make technology equipment and servers. Gou noted his company\u2019s history in Harrisburg. He said the goal was to create \u201chigh-precision, high-tech, high value-added manufacturing in the U.S.\u201d</p> <p>Gou also said Foxconn would donate $10\u00a0million to Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh for robotics research.</p> <p>\u201cIPhone maker Foxconn to invest $40\u00a0million in the US,\u201d wrote the China Post. \u201cFoxconn to build plant in Pennsylvania,\u201d CNN said. Local media quoted state officials as saying Gou would sign a memorandum of understanding and the company was busy scouting sites.</p> <p>\u201cThere was this intense buzz,\u201d recalled David Black, president of the Harrisburg Regional Chamber.</p> <p>\u201cLots of us were excited by the opportunity,\u201d said Bub Manning, who at the time co-owned a local construction firm. \u201cAnytime something of that size comes along in central Pennsylvania, that\u2019s a big deal.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela8.JPG?uuid=WYomYOp3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela8.JPG?uuid=WYomYOp3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela8.JPG?uuid=WYomYOp3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/02/04/Others/Images/2017-02-03/MarkMakela8.JPG?uuid=WYomYOp3EeaQPZsR7X2NKg\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> In 2013, Foxconn\u2019s chairman sent a jolt through this state capital when he said his company \u2014 best known for making Apple iPhones in China \u2014 would invest $30\u00a0million and hire 500 workers for a new high-tech factory in central Pennsylvania. (Mark Makela/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>For years, Foxconn has maintained an office with about 50 workers in Harrisburg, a hub of the computer connector industry. The office is in an old brick warehouse. On a recent visit, the building was filled by a half-empty warren of cubicles. A worker declined to comment to a reporter with The Washington Post. According to local government officials, Foxconn has not done any permitted alterations to the building in 15\u00a0years.</p> <p>Manning recalled how his firm tried to chase down information about the planned $30\u00a0million factory.</p> <p>\u201cIt felt like I was grabbing Jell-O. It never got any traction,\u201d Manning said.</p> <p>The $30\u00a0million factory faded from view.</p> <p>Foxconn said its $10\u00a0million donation to Carnegie Mellon was \u201cmoving forward very successfully,\u201d with half of the funds having been spent four years later. The school declined to comment.</p> <p>None of this has stopped the praise for Foxconn\u2019s investment in Pennsylvania. Recent stories have referred to the phantom factory as a sign of Foxconn\u2019s U.S. beachhead. A state economic development official wrote about Foxconn\u2019s $30\u00a0million factory as evidence of foreign companies that operate in the Keystone State. (The piece was fixed after The Washington Post inquired about it.)</p> <p>State officials toured potential sites with Foxconn representatives, the state Department of Community and Economic Development said in a statement. \u201cUnfortunately, a project did not come to fruition.\u201d Foxconn attributed the failure to \u201cmaterial changes to the business and operating climate at that time.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cThe proof is always in the pudding,\u201d said Scott Andes, a senior policy analyst at Brookings.</p> <p>The assumption is that companies follow through on major announcements. \u201cBut it is important to be skeptical,\u201d Andes said. \u201cI would hope it\u2019s not just smoke and mirrors with Foxconn or any other of these companies.\u201d</p> <p>Feeling burned by the fate of the $30\u00a0million factory, some in Harrisburg are cautious about getting their hopes up again.</p> <p>\u201cWe learned something from that,\u201d said Black of the Harrisburg chamber.</p> <p>But, Manning said, it would be hard to not be seduced by the size of Foxconn\u2019s ambition, real or not.</p> <p>\u201cIf you smelled a project like that,\u201d he said, \u201cyou\u2019d get excited even though you know better.\u201d</p> <p></p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <b>Read more:</b> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\">
