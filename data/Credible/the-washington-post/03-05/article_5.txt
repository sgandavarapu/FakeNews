data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_960w/2010-2019/Wires/Images/2017-02-21/AP/Immigration_Enforcement_79455.jpg-cd943.jpg&amp;w=1484\" <span class=\"pb-caption\">An arrest is made during an Immigration and Customs Enforcement enforcement operation in Los Angeles on Feb. 7. (Charles Reed/U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement via Associated Press)</span> </div> <p>Tens of thousands of immigrants detained by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement were forced to work for $1 day, or for nothing at all \u2014 a violation of federal anti-slavery laws \u2014 a lawsuit claims.</p> <p>The lawsuit, filed in 2014 against one of the largest private prison companies in the country, reached class-action status this week after a federal judge\u2019s ruling. That means the case could involve as many as 60,000 immigrants who have been detained.</p> <p>It\u2019s the first time a class-action lawsuit accusing a private U.S. prison company of forced labor has been allowed to move forward.</p> <p>\u201cThat\u2019s obviously a big deal; it\u2019s recognizing the possibility that a government contractor could be engaging in forced labor,\u201d said Nina DiSalvo, executive director of Towards Justice, a Colorado-based nonprofit group that represents low-wage workers, including undocumented immigrants. \u201cCertification of the class is perhaps the only mechanism by which these vulnerable individuals who were dispersed across the country and across the world would ever be able to vindicate their rights.\u201d</p> <p>At the heart of the dispute is <a href=\"https://www.ice.gov/detention-facility/denver-contract-detention-facility\" target=\"_blank\">the Denver Contract Detention Facility</a>, a 1,500-bed center in Aurora, Colo., owned and operated by GEO Group under a contract with ICE. The Florida-based corporation runs facilities to house immigrants who are awaiting their turn in court.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/this-company-is-making-millions-from-americas-broken-immigration-system/2017/03/08/43abce9e-f881-11e6-be05-1a3817ac21a5_story.html?utm_term=.c7b147609f43\">This company is making millions from America\u2019s broken immigration system</a>] </i> </p> <p>The lawsuit, filed against GEO Group on behalf of nine immigrants, initially sought more than $5 million in damages. Attorneys expect the damages to grow substantially given the case\u2019s new class-action status.</p> <p>The class-action ruling by U.S. District Judge John Kane means that as many as 60,000 current and former detainees at the detention facility in Aurora are now part of the lawsuit without having to actively join as plaintiffs, said Andrew Free, one of the plaintiffs\u2019 attorneys</p> <p>The lead plaintiff in the case is a permanent resident of the U.S., and attorneys expect \u201ca significant portion of the class will fit that bill,\u201d Free said.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/wonk/wp/2016/12/01/after-review-of-policy-homeland-security-panel-says-private-immigrant-detention-will-continue/?utm_term=.fc1b0d9f5d27\">In policy review, Homeland Security panel sends mixed message about future of private immigrant detention</a>] </i> </p> <p>The original nine plaintiffs claim that detainees at the ICE facility are forced to work without pay \u2014 and that those who refuse to do so are threatened with solitary confinement.</p> <p>Specifically, the lawsuit claims, six detainees are selected at random every day and are forced to clean the facility\u2019s housing units. The lawsuit claims that the practice violates the federal Trafficking Victims Protection Act, which prohibits modern-day slavery.</p> <p>\u201cForced labor is a particular violation of the statute that we\u2019ve alleged,\u201d Free said. \u201cWhether you\u2019re calling it forced labor or slavery, the practical reality for the plaintiffs is much the same. You\u2019re being compelled to work against your will under the threat of force or use of force.\u201d</p> <p>GEO Group also is accused of violating Colorado\u2019s minimum wage laws by paying detainees $1 day instead of the state\u2019s minimum wage of about $9 an hour. The company \u201cunjustly enriched\u201d itself through the cheap labor of detainees, the lawsuit says.</p> <p>None of the original nine plaintiffs are still detained at the facility, DiSalvo said.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-right\" style=\"width:273px;\"> <a name=\"8977ced386\"></a> <img alt=\"ICE's Denver Contract Detention Facility. (U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement)\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/news/post-nation/wp-content/uploads/sites/23/2017/03/denverice.jpg\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">ICE\u2019s Denver Contract Detention Facility. (U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement)</span> </div> <p><span style=\"font-family: 'Georgia',serif; color: #333333;\">The class-action ruling by Kane, a senior judge in the U.S. District Court in Colorado,\u00a0</span><span style=\"font-family: 'Georgia',serif; color: #333333;\">came at a critical time, DiSalvo said, noting President Trump\u2019s <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/the-fix/wp/2016/11/13/donald-trump-plans-to-immediately-deport-2-to-3-million-undocumented-immigrants/?utm_term=.e92e2fd4f920\">pledge to deport 2 million to 3 million undocumented immigrants</a>. Advocates say private prison companies that have government contracts stand to benefit significantly from the president\u2019s hard-line policy of detaining and deporting a massive number of immigrants</span></p> <p><span style=\"font-family: 'Georgia',serif; color: #333333;\">\u201cThat means you need to round up and detain more people in order to determine whether they have the rights to stay in this country before you deport them,\u201d DiSalvo said. \u201cMore people could be moving through, not just in the Aurora facility. More people could be subjected to GEO\u2019s forced labor policy.\u201d</span></p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"160618\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Trump administration strengthens immigration enforcement guidelines\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58accc1be4b04fcea7a68c34\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/02-21-2017/t_1487720164110_name_20170221_trump_thumbnail.jpg\" data-uuid=\"dbd92ed6-f88c-11e6-aa1e-5f735ee31334\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"Do1SfR-dRIU\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">The Trump administration on Feb. 21 issued guidelines strengthening enforcement against illegal immigration but insisted that it isn't seeking \"mass deportations.\" (Bastien Inzaurralde/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p style=\"text-align: start;\"><span style=\"font-family: 'Georgia',serif; color: #333333;\">Notably, the stocks of the two biggest private prison operators, Geo Group and CoreCivic (formerly known as Corrections Corporation of America), <a href=\"http://money.cnn.com/2017/02/24/investing/private-prison-stocks-soar-trump/\">have surged since Trump\u2019s election</a>. The companies donated a total of $500,000 to Trump\u2019s inaugural festivities, <a href=\"http://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2017/02/23/private-prisons-back-trump-and-could-see-big-payoffs-new-policies/98300394/\">USA Today reported</a>. Since Trump took office, his\u00a0</span><span style=\"font-family: 'Georgia',serif; color: #333333;\">administration has reversed the Obama administration\u2019s policy to end the country\u2019s reliance on private prisons. </span></p> <p>GEO Group has strongly denied the lawsuit\u2019s allegations and argued in court records that pay of $1 a day does not violate any laws.</p> <p>\u201cWe intend to continue to vigorously defend our company against these claims,\u201d GEO Group spokesman Pablo Paez said in a statement. \u201cThe volunteer work program at immigration facilities as well as the wage rates and standards associated with the program are set by the Federal government. Our facilities, including the Aurora, Colo. Facility, are highly rated and provide high-quality services in safe, secure, and humane residential environments pursuant to the Federal Government\u2019s national standards.\u201d</p> <p>Jennifer D. Elzea, acting press sectrary for ICE, said she couldn\u2019t comment on the litigation because \u201cICE is not specifically a party in this suit.\u201d</p> <p>Under ICE\u2019s <a href=\"https://www.ice.gov/doclib/detention-standards/2011/5-8.pdf\">Voluntary Work Program</a>, detainees sign up to work and are paid $1 a day. The nationwide program, ICE says, \u201cprovides detainees opportunities to work and earn money while confined, subject to the number of work opportunities available and within the constraints of the safety, security and good order of the facility.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-politics/wp/2017/02/23/trump-touts-recent-immigration-raids-calls-them-a-military-operation/?utm_term=.abd072313105\">Trump touts recent immigration raids, calls them a \u2018military operation\u2019</a>] </i> </p> <p>Detainees work for up to eight\u00a0hours a day, 40 hours a week, cleaning bathrooms, showers, toilets, windows, patient rooms and staff offices, waxing floors, and preparing and serving meals. ICE says detainees \u201cshall be able to volunteer for work assignments but otherwise shall not be required to work, except to do personal housekeeping.\u201d</p> <p>Jacqueline Stevens, who runs Northwestern University\u2019s Deportation Research Clinic, said the program does not meet the criteria for what qualifies as volunteer work under labor laws.</p> <p>\u201cJust slapping the word \u2018volunteer\u2019 in front of \u2018work program\u2019 doesn\u2019t exempt the prison firm from paying legally mandated wages any more than McDonald\u2019s can use \u2018volunteer\u2019 senior citizens and pay them Big Macs,\u201d said Stevens, whose research about the volunteer work program prompted the lawsuit.</p> <p>Prison labor, Stevens added, has two purposes: to punish prisoners after they\u2019ve been convicted of a crime and to rehabilitate them.</p> <p>Those don\u2019t apply to immigrant detainees, she said.</p> <p>\u201cThere\u2019s no ostensible purpose to rehabilitate them,\u201d Stevens said. \u201cThey\u2019re just waiting for a court date in order to clarify their immigration status. Some don\u2019t end up being deported.\u201d</p> <p>Free, one of the plaintiffs\u2019 attorneys, said there are alternatives to detaining immigrants while they wait for their day in court. That includes supervision programs and community monitoring.</p> <p>\u201cThat\u2019s much cheaper than spending double the current cost of detention,\u201d Free said, adding that not incarcerating them would ensure they\u2019re able to find attorneys and attend their immigration hearings. \u201cThe for-profit prisons are a policy choice against due process in immigration courts and against access to counsel and against positive outcomes to immigrants who have valid claims.\u201d</p> <p>In 2014, GEO Group filed a motion to dismiss the lawsuit, arguing in court records that Colorado\u2019s minimum wage law does not apply to immigrant detainees.</p> <p>\u201cDetainees are not whom the minimum wage laws were intended to protect. The minimum wage law was enacted in Colorado to ensure wages are adequate to \u2018supply the necessary cost of living and to maintain the health of workers so employed,'\u201d the attorneys argued, quoting the state statute.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/justice-department-will-again-use-private-prisons/2017/02/23/da395d02-fa0e-11e6-be05-1a3817ac21a5_story.html?utm_term=.d077b25c7ba2\">Justice Department will again use private prisons</a>] </i> </p> <p>The company further argued that the Trafficking Victims Protection Act is inapplicable because the law is meant to prevent human trafficking of people for labor and/or sex. GEO Group, the attorneys wrote, \u201cdid not traffic Plaintiffs in the Aurora facility with the purpose of putting them to work.\u201d They added that the detainees are in the custody of immigration officials.</p> <p>In 2015, Kane, the federal judge, partially denied the motion to dismiss. Although he agreed with GEO Group that Colorado\u2019s minimum wage law is inapplicable, he ruled that the other claims can stand.</p> <p>\u201cGEO\u2019s argument was, \u2018Even if we are forcing people to work under threat of solitary confinement, that would be allowed,'\u201d DiSalvo said. \u201cAnd the judge said, \u2018No it wouldn\u2019t be.'\u201d</p> <p>Kane granted class-action status a few days after the Justice Department directed the Bureau of Prisons to, again, use private prisons, a significant shift from the <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-nation/wp/2016/08/18/justice-department-says-it-will-end-use-of-private-prisons/?utm_term=.39e60002e9f7\">Obama-era policy</a> of significantly reducing \u2014 and ultimately ending \u2014 their use.</p> <p>In a one-paragraph memo last week, Attorney General Jeff Sessions rescinded the previous directive to the Bureau of Prisons to either reduce or decline to renew private-prison contracts as they came due, <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/justice-department-will-again-use-private-prisons/2017/02/23/da395d02-fa0e-11e6-be05-1a3817ac21a5_story.html?utm_term=.d077b25c7ba2\">The Washington Post\u2019s Matt Zapotosky reported</a>.</p> <p>\u201cThe memorandum changed long-standing policy and practice, and impaired the Bureau\u2019s ability to meet the future needs of the federal correctional system,\u201d Sessions wrote. \u201cTherefore, I direct the Bureau to return to its previous approach.\u201d</p> <p>The original directive from the Obama administration did not apply to immigration detainees.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-gallery-embedded\"> <link href=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/pb/gr/c/ssiSingle/rO609L1qgAmx7p/css/b0fa517a2c.css?_=0c4a1\"
