Here's what you need to know about the plan. (Bastien Inzaurralde,Victoria Walker,Sarah Parnass/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>When House Republicans unveiled on Monday a <a href=\"http://www.readthebill.gop\">plan\u00a0to replace the Affordable Care Act</a>, one thing was immediately clear: the new approach could create a major shift in taxes for low-and-middle income people\u00a0while delivering a $600 billion\u00a0tax break, primarily to the rich.</p> <p>Republicans have long criticized the ACA\u00a0for creating a whole new system of tax credits\u00a0based on individual income levels intended to help people buy health insurance on established health-care exchanges. The new plan replaces those credits with a fresh series of tax subsidies\u00a0based on age and income that are intended to make it easier to buy insurance on the private market.</p> <p>The\u00a0GOP proposal would also eliminate the complex web of tax increases established by Obamacare on people and companies \u2014 everything from \u00a0tanning salons to medical device manufacturers \u2014 that help pay for benefits in the current law, like free breast cancer screenings, preventative care, and allowing people to remain on their parents\u2019 insurance until the age of 26. They\u2019d also help underwrite the cost of prohibiting insurers from banning those with pre-existing conditions.</p> <p>The combination of maintaining the most popular and well-known portions of Obamacare \u2014 and cutting taxes \u2014 is expected to cost\u00a0the federal government as much as $600 billion over ten years, <a href=\"https://www.jct.gov/publications.html?func=startdown&amp;id=4988\">according to the non-partisan Joint Committee on Taxation.</a></p> <p>But, Republicans\u00a0say, their plan is a silver bullet aimed at remedying years of unfair tax increases and uneven health coverage for people across the income brackets. House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wisc.) called the GOP plan a chance to \u201cequalize the tax treatment of health care for all Americans.\u201d</p> <p><a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/house-leaders-brace-for-the-task-ahead-selling-obamacare-lite/2017/03/07/ab2f721e-02e5-11e7-ad5b-d22680e18d10_story.html?hpid=hp_rhp-top-table-main_repeal-710a%3Ahomepage%2Fstory\" target=\"_blank\">McConnell says Senate will take up House\u2019s Obamacare replacement measure</a></p> <p>So far, Republicans have not released critical details to back up those promises. They have not revealed\u00a0any detailed accounting on\u00a0how different income and age groups will be affected by the GOP plan and how much the plan will cost the government. That information is\u00a0typically revealed\u00a0along with any major legislation. But GOP leaders have not yet said when they will make it\u00a0public.</p> <p>Here\u2019s what we do know: The GOP plan would provide major tax breaks for the wealthiest one percent of the population. Low-income people are most likely to see their credits slashed while middle-income earners could have access to bigger, better credits down the line.</p> <p><a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/the-fix/wp/2017/03/07/the-gop-is-walking-into-an-obamacare-minefield-right-now/?hpid=hp_rhp-top-table-main_fix-minefield-1120a:homepage/story&amp;utm_term=.aedbf140b41c&amp;tid=a_inl\" target=\"_blank\">The GOP is walking into an Obamacare minefield right now</a></p> <p>Taxpayers should also prepare for more tax changes in the future as Republicans plan to stall\u00a0a tax on high-value employer coverage\u00a0known as \u201cCadillac plans.\u201d That planned 40 percent tax on pricey health-care plans may be necessary to offset the cost of those popular benefits that Republicans have vowed to protect.</p> <p>There are also still many unanswered questions\u00a0about whether the GOP approach can avoid creating a huge budget deficit over the long haul. Democrats and some conservatives don\u2019t necessarily believe the math will add up.</p> <p><strong>Here are five ways the GOP proposal would change the tax system:</strong></p> <p><strong>1. This is a major tax cut for the rich.\u00a0</strong>House Republicans plan to eliminate nearly every tax increase that was included included in the ACA, many of which were levied on\u00a0high-income earners. Democrats insist that some tax increases were necessary to offset the cost of expanding coverage and overhauling the health-care system to ensure people received a minimum level of coverage.</p> <p>Republicans say they\u2019ve found a way to pay for wider coverage that doesn\u2019t include taxes on the on the rich, which are the\u00a0backbone of the current system. The new GOP plan <a href=\"https://waysandmeans.house.gov/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/03.06.17-Section-by-Section.pdf\">would repeal</a> two\u00a0of the biggest-ticket taxes in the ACA: a 3.8 percent tax on investment income; and a 0.9 percent levy on income over $200,000 for individuals and $250, 000 for married couples filing jointly. Those two taxes hit fewer than four million households\u00a0making up\u00a0the top 2.5 percent of taxpayers, according to IRS data.</p> <p>\u201cThose taxes raise over $300 billion over a decade,\u201d said Scott Greenberg, an analyst at the conservative-leaning Tax Foundation \u201cThe impacts\u00a0of those taxes are largely on high-income households.\u201d</p> <p>Wealthy earners would also benefit from a rule to halve the penalty on using money from a Health Savings Accounts on expenses not related to health care. Current limits allow individuals to directly transfer up to\u00a0$3,400, and families up to $6,750, into an HSA without paying any taxes on the income.</p> <p>\u201cThey\u2019re very attractive to highly compensated individuals who have already maxed out other tax-preferred retirement plans,\u201d said Gordon Mermin, a senior research associate at the Tax Policy Center. \u201cThey can use it in retirement as a retirement account. If it turns out they have health expenses and use it for that, then they\u2019re not paying tax at all.\u201d</p> <p>Wealthy people\u00a0already make up the majority of people using the accounts. Families earning over $60,000 made\u00a0up nearly\u00a065 percent of the total that contributed in 2014, according to <a href=\"https://www.treasury.gov/resource-center/tax-policy/tax-analysis/Documents/HSA-Tables.pdf\">recent data from the Treasury Department</a>. \u00a0Nearly two-thirds of those people earned between $75,000 and $200,000.</p> <p><strong>2. Lower-income workers are going to feel the biggest squeeze.\u00a0</strong>The poorest workers would see a nearly opposite scenario under the GOP plan. Most estimates say they would be the hardest hit.</p> <p>Low-income\u00a0people traditionally do not participate in the Health Savings Account program and often rely more heavily on the existing tax subsidies.</p> <p>\u201c<span style=\"font-weight: 400;\">The folks who lose the most under this proposal are the low income people who qualify for a relatively large tax credit today,\u201d Mermin said. \u201cIt\u00a0</span><span style=\"font-weight: 400;\">will either mean they will be paying a lot more for their plans or buying a plan that covers a lot less.\u00a0</span>They could wind up with\u00a0a much higher deductible.\u201d</p> <p><a href=\"https://waysandmeans.house.gov/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/03.06.17-Section-by-Section.pdf\">The GOP\u00a0plan calls for\u00a0tax credits</a> ranging from $2,000 per year for those under 30 to $4,000 per year for those over 60.\u00a0The full credit would be available for individuals earning up to $75,000 a year and up to $150,000 for married couples filing jointly. The credits would phase out for individuals earning more \u2014 for each $1,000 in additional income, a person would be entitled to $100 less in credit, meaning a 61-year old could make up to $115,000 and still receive some credit.</p> <p>But Mermin and other analysts said that most low-income people, regardless of age, receive much higher payments under the current law.</p> <p>\u201cThis new health insurance credit is likely to be less helpful for low-income folks but more helpful for those\u00a0who currently don\u2019t benefit from the current premium tax credit,\u201d Mermin said.</p> <p>The plan could also have major impact on people who might want to use\u00a0Medicaid in the 31 states and the District of Columbia that accepted expanded ACA benefits. Starting in 2020, those states would only receive the full federal reimbursement for people who qualified for the program by that date. The rest of the population would be subject to strict new per-capita caps on how much each state would receive for Medicaid.</p> <p><strong>3. There may be more tax changes to come.\u00a0</strong>The question of the day in GOP circle is what will happen to a 40 percent tax on expensive employer-provided insurance, known as the <a href=\"http://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/issue-briefs/2016/june/cadillac-tax\">Cadillac Tax.</a> House Republicans decided to delay the start of that unpopular but valuable tax from its scheduled implementation date of 2020 to\u00a02025.</p> <p>That five-year delay may not seem significant. But some tax experts say it looks like a convenient way to make the legislation more appealing to conservatives today while hiding a tax increase that could be coming in the future.</p> <p>\u201c<span style=\"font-weight: 400;\">It certainly looks like the type of provision you\u2019d put in the bill if you\u2019re worried about long- term deficits rather than short-term deficits,\u201d Greenberg said.</span></p> <p><strong>4. All of these tax changes could create a big deficit headache. \u00a0</strong>Some conservatives would rather see the \u201cCadillac Tax\u201d disappear entirely \u2014 but that would risk violating Senate rules. Republican leaders chose to use a quirk in the budget rules to vote on the health-care law without the threat of a blockade by Democrats. The unusual process means the bill needs only 51 votes for passage \u2014 but only if it doesn\u2019t add to the deficit after 10 years.</p> <p>Adding the Cadillac Tax back in 2025 could help Republicans balance the budget. But only if they don\u2019t decide to delay it again in 2025.</p> <p>Republicans aren\u2019t exactly denying this might be a budget gimmick<strong>.</strong> Some GOP lawmakers and staffers have privately acknowledged they\u2019re delaying the \u201cCadillac Tax\u201d to push the increase off as far as possible without creating an even bigger hole in the deficit.</p> <p>\u201cWe don\u2019t know if that\u2019s going to be in the final combination of this thing or not \u2014 there\u2019s still a lot of conversation we need to have,\u201d Sen. David Perdue (R-Ga.) told reporters on Tuesday.</p> <p><strong>5. Democrats and even some conservatives are skeptical the math will work out in Republicans\u2019 favor.</strong>\u00a0The GOP plan was already under attack from both sides of the political spectrum from the moment it was released. On the one hand, Democrats are betting the GOP plan will fail because the tax changes would cost too much<strong>.</strong> On the other, conservatives are\u00a0calling the plan \u201cObamacare lite\u201d for replacing the old system of tax credits with a new one\u00a0and leaving intact the \u201cCadillac Tax.\u201d</p> <p>Democrats argue\u00a0that cutting tax hikes on the wealthy and eliminating penalties on employers who don\u2019t provide health insurance \u2014 and individuals who don\u2019t buy coverage \u2014 makes it impossible to pay for the parts of the law people do like. They say insurance companies will raise prices if there aren\u2019t enough employers offering coverage and individuals decide not to buy insurance.\u00a0Insurance companies\u00a0won\u2019t have any other way of paying for expensive benefits.</p> <p>\u201cThis is not an honest or responsible attempt at legislating, but rather a devastating future for millions of hard-working Americans,\u201d said Rep. Richard Neal (D-Mass.) the top Democrat on the Ways and Means Committee. \u201cThe Republican repeal bill would give huge tax cuts to the wealthiest Americans \u2013 including health insurance companies and their CEOs \u2013 while reducing health care tax credits for millions of low-income Americans and seniors.\u201d</p> <p>Conservatives say they can\u2019t back a bill that leaves any vestiges of Obamacare intact. Members of the influential House Freedom Caucus and outside groups like the Club for Growth all slammed the House GOP plan for\u00a0replacing old subsidies with new tax credits.</p> <p><a href=\"Conservatives rebel against GOP\u2019s ACA bill\">\u2018Obamacare Lite,\u2019 \u2018RINOcare': Conservatives rebel against GOP\u2019s ACA bill</a></p> <p>\u201cIt creates a new entitlement through the refundable tax credits,\u201d said FreedomWorks policy analyst Jason Pye said in a statement. \u201cIt allows insurance companies to assess a 30 percent penalty on those who don\u2019t keep continuous coverage for 63 days, which is an individual mandate by another name.\u201d</p> <p>\u00a0</p> <link href=\"//css.washingtonpost.com/wp-stat/sub/css/wp-story-style.css\"
