(Joe Raedle/Getty Images)</span> </div> <p>Major associations representing physicians, hospitals, insurers and seniors all leveled sharp attacks against the House GOP\u2019s plan to rewrite the Affordable Care Act on Wednesday, as some Republicans publicly questioned whether the measure can clear the House of Representatives.</p> <p>While industry groups warned that the proposal could leave vulnerable Americans with fewer protections than they now have, GOP leaders pressed ahead, bringing legislation before two key committees that are expected to approve the bills by week\u2019s end. They were also working in concert with the White House to win over conservatives, who have complained that the proposal preserves too much of the current law. </p> <p>The flurry of activity \u2014 including an evening meeting between President Trump and leaders from five skeptical conservative groups \u2014 created new uncertainty about the viability of Republicans\u2019 signature promise to repeal and replace Obamacare.</p> <p>The day\u2019s events also showed the uneasy predicament facing House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wis.), the proposal\u2019s chief booster, who described the plan Wednesday as a \u201cconservative wish list\u201d that would deliver on years of GOP campaign promises to change the nation\u2019s health-care system.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"166442\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Republicans offer a mix of criticism and support for health-care plan\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58bf0f89e4b059a9e4239d83\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-09-2017/t_1489067707174_name_Attorney_General_Russia_03795_0d574.jpg\" data-uuid=\"a0b10048-036f-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"-lbugR1zqQA\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">(Sarah Parnass/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>\u201cRight now I feel confident saying there aren\u2019t 218 votes for this,\u201d said Rep. Scott Perry (R-Pa.), a member of the conservative Freedom Caucus, who was referring to the number of votes needed to pass the measure out of the House. Perry opposes the proposal.</p> <p>The barrage of criticism shows how fraught the terrain of health-care policy is. It also reflects a backlash prompted at least partly by the breakneck speed with which House Republicans are trying to push through their proposal \u2014 with little upfront effort to work with interest groups or political factions.</p> <p>\u201cWhat we\u2019re seeing now is that the political prospects for repealing the Affordable Care Act are as daunting as the effort to pass national health reform,\u201d Larry Jacobs, a political science professor at the University of Minnesota\u2019s Hubert H. Humphrey School of Public Affairs, said in an interview.</p> <p>House Republican leaders have given little indication that they will make anything but marginal changes to their plan, which would eliminate the requirements that all Americans obtain coverage or pay a tax penalty and that businesses with at least 50 employees provide insurance. The American Health Care Act would replace income-based subsidies with refundable tax credits based on age and income, charge individuals a 30\u00a0percent surcharge if they buy a plan after allowing their coverage to lapse and phase out the law\u2019s more generous Medicaid funding over time.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"72706\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"White House promises 'full court press' on health-care plan as criticism mounts\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58c07941e4b01a7d50403baf\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-08-2017/t_1489009521345_name_Trump_61166_28306.jpg\" data-uuid=\"3a338196-0447-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"05tKKlTMHZE\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">(The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>While conservatives complained that these changes don\u2019t go far enough, they have sparked criticism not just from Democrats but from moderate Republicans, AARP, the American Medical Association and the American Hospital Association. </p> <p>\u201cWe cannot support the AHCA as drafted because of the expected decline in health insurance coverage and the potential harm it would cause to vulnerable patient populations,\u201d James L. Madara, chief executive of the American Medical Association and a doctor, wrote in a letter to committee leaders overseeing work on the bill.</p> <p>Richard Pollack, CEO of the American Hospital Association, voiced similar fears, saying efforts to \u201crestructure the Medicaid program\u201d by shifting it from an entitlement program to one based on a per capita allocation \u201cwill have the effect of making significant reductions in a program that provides services for our most vulnerable populations and already pays providers significantly less than the cost of providing care.\u201d</p> <p>America\u2019s Health Insurance Plans, the insurance industry\u2019s largest trade association, sent a letter Wednesday saying that while it appreciated several of the proposed changes, the changes to Medicaid \u201ccould result in unnecessary disruptions in the coverage and care beneficiaries depend on.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"http://wapo.st/2mxXC6t\" shape=\"rect\">Income separates the winners from losers in House GOP\u2019s health plan</a>]</i> </p> <p>House Energy and Commerce Committee Chairman Greg Walden (R-Ore.) told reporters Wednesday that critics were exaggerating the proposal\u2019s potential repercussions.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s tough to do entitlement reform, it\u2019s tough to make these changes, but I think at the end of day, seniors are going to be fine,\u201d Walden said. \u201cIf you\u2019re on Medicaid today, you\u2019ll be on Medicaid tomorrow. States can intercede here and help out. So there\u2019s more to this story.\u201d</p> <p>Walden said it was \u201csort of shocking\u201d that hospital groups were strongly opposing the plan, because the GOP legislation restores the ACA\u2019s cuts to \u201cdisproportionate share\u201d payments to hospitals that serve large numbers of uninsured patients. </p> <p>\u201cThere\u2019s a pretty big medical-industrial complex in America,\u201d he added. \u201cAnd when you touch it, I\u2019ve discovered, it touches back.\u201d</p> <p>House Republicans\u2019 determination to deliver on their promise to undo the ACA \u2014 Ryan said Wednesday that it is \u201cthe covenant we made with the American people when we ran on a repeal-and-replace plan in 2016\u201d \u2014 has spurred a legislative drive that is happening at warp speed.</p> <p>A cadre of lawmakers and staffers worked behind closed doors for several weeks to draft the pair of bills, which were designed to move through the annual budget process in order to clear the Senate with a simple majority vote.</p> <p>But that process, which did not involve an extended period of negotiation with interest groups or consultation with Democrats, has produced a furious backlash. </p> <p>Some insurers, including Molina Healthcare and the Alliance of Community Health Plans, said they did not get to offer any input into the House proposal.</p> <p>\u201cIt doesn\u2019t seem like the industry got any heads up or was involved. We definitely were not,\u201d Sunny Yu, spokeswoman for Molina Healthcare, which has about a million members in the Affordable Care Act exchanges, said in an email.</p> <p>Democrats threw up procedural obstacles Wednesday in the committee meetings and on the House floor, complaining that it was irresponsible to consider the bills before the Congressional Budget Office offered an analysis that showed the legislation\u2019s impact on the budget and Americans\u2019 overall health care coverage.</p> <p>\u201cWe need to know, what this is going to cost?\u201d asked Rep. Anna G. Eshoo (D-Calif.), a member of the Energy and Commerce Committee. \u201cWe need to know, what kind of health insurance is going to be feasible?\u201d</p> <p>Both House Republicans and White House budget director Mick Mulvaney have predicted that they will have a CBO \u201cscore\u201d ready by early next week, before the bills are combined and brought before the House Budget Committee. GOP staffers noted that other health-care bills, including the 21st Century Cures Act and the 2015 reauthorization bill for Medicare and the Children\u2019s Health Insurance Program, began their journeys through congressional committees without a CBO score.</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p>Democrats on the Energy and Commerce Committee moved to delay the bill\u2019s consideration for 30 days, while those on Ways and Means moved to delay it for one week to allow for further hearings and to examine the CBO report. Both motions were voted down on a straight party-line vote, and the panels continued working into the evening. </p> <p>Still, the most imminent threat GOP leaders must contend with comes from the far right. The speaker can lose only 21 Republican votes if the American Health Care Act is to pass, and opponents are promising to use that leverage to force changes to the bill. </p> <p>Rep. Thomas Garrett (R-Va.), a freshman member of the conservative House Freedom Caucus, said he was confident that his camp could help reshape the legislation. While Trump had endorsed it already, Garrett said, he would probably be willing to accept something else if it were changed in a way conservatives could support.</p> <p>\u201cThis is a guy who said he wasn\u2019t sure that NATO, in its classic role, is necessary \u2014 and then two months later we saw countries like Germany vow to increase their defense spending to 2\u00a0percent of GDP,\u201d said Garrett. \u201cWhy\u2019d they do that? Because Donald Trump wasn\u2019t going to accept the status quo. They made a counteroffer. Right now, there\u2019s an offer, and he\u2019s saying he likes to get people to make counteroffers.\u201d</p> <p>Vice President Pence met with two House Freedom Caucus leaders Tuesday, and that same day Mulvaney \u2014 a former caucus member \u2014 spent more than an hour at a meeting of the group. Its members have been invited to visit the White House next Tuesday.</p> <p>Wednesday night, Trump met with leaders from Americans for Prosperity, the Club for Growth, FreedomWorks, the Heritage Foundation and its political arm, Heritage Action, and the Tea Party Patriots.</p> <p>But the hard reality for Republicans is that any changes made to appease House conservatives could threaten the bill\u2019s support among moderates.</p> <p>\u201cMy fear is that the bill will go backwards,\u201d said Rep. Tom MacArthur (R-N.J.), a co-chairman of the centrist Tuesday Group who has gotten White House attention of his own: He met Tuesday with Health and Human Services Secretary Tom Price. \u201cIf the bill starts going in the reverse direction in order to satisfy certain members of my party, then I\u2019m going to have a problem. I think the federal government has a role to play here, and I\u2019m not looking to just see the federal government undermine the health-care needs of the American people.\u201d</p> <p>Sen. Susan Collins of Maine, a pivotal Republican moderate, said in <a href=\"https://www.yahoo.com/katiecouric/841841-175147366.html\" shape=\"rect\">an interview </a>Wednesday with Yahoo News\u2019 Katie Couric that the current House measure would \u201cnot be well received in the Senate\u201d and stood no chance of passing as is. \u201cI want us to slow down to take more time to be sure we get this right.\u201d</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>David Weigel, Carolyn Johnson, Elise Viebeck and Amy Goldstein contributed to this report.</p> </p> <div They were also working in concert with the White House to win over conservatives, who have complained that the proposal preserves too much of the current law.\n\nThe flurry of activity \u2014 including an evening meeting between President Trump and leaders from five skeptical conservative groups \u2014 created new uncertainty about the viability of Republicans\u2019 signature promise to repeal and replace Obamacare.\n\nThe day\u2019s events also showed the uneasy predicament facing House Speaker Paul D. Perry opposes the proposal.\n\nThe barrage of criticism shows how fraught the terrain of health-care policy is. It also reflects a backlash prompted at least partly by the breakneck speed with which House Republicans are trying to push through their proposal \u2014 with little upfront effort to work with interest groups or political factions.\n\n\u201cWhat we\u2019re seeing now is that the political prospects for repealing the Affordable Care Act are as daunting as the effort to pass national health reform,\u201d Larry Jacobs, a political science professor at the University of Minnesota\u2019s Hubert H. States can intercede here and help out. So there\u2019s more to this story.\u201d\n\nWalden said it was \u201csort of shocking\u201d that hospital groups were strongly opposing the plan, because the GOP legislation restores the ACA\u2019s cuts to \u201cdisproportionate share\u201d payments to hospitals that serve large numbers of uninsured patients.\n\n\u201cThere\u2019s a pretty big medical-industrial complex in America,\u201d he added. \u201cAnd when you touch it, I\u2019ve discovered, it touches back.\u201d\n\nHouse Republicans\u2019 determination to deliver on their promise to undo the ACA \u2014 Ryan said Wednesday that it is \u201cthe covenant we made with the American people when we ran on a repeal-and-replace plan in 2016\u201d \u2014 has spurred a legislative drive that is happening at warp speed.\n\nA cadre of lawmakers and staffers worked behind closed doors for several weeks to draft the pair of bills, which were designed to move through the annual budget process in order to clear the Senate with a simple majority vote.\n\nBut that process, which did not involve an extended period of negotiation with interest groups or consultation with Democrats, has produced a furious backlash.\n\nSome insurers, including Molina Healthcare and the Alliance of Community Health Plans, said they did not get to offer any input into the House proposal.\n\n\u201cIt doesn\u2019t seem like the industry got any heads up or was involved. We definitely were not,\u201d Sunny Yu, spokeswoman for Molina Healthcare, which has about a million members in the Affordable Care Act exchanges, said in an email.\n\nDemocrats threw up procedural obstacles Wednesday in the committee meetings and on the House floor, complaining that it was irresponsible to consider the bills before the Congressional Budget Office offered an analysis that showed the legislation\u2019s impact on the budget and Americans\u2019 overall health care coverage.\n\n\u201cWe need to know, what this is going to cost?\u201d asked Rep. Anna G. Eshoo (D-Calif.), a member of the Energy and Commerce Committee. \u201cWe need to know, what kind of health insurance is going to be feasible?\u201d\n\nBoth House Republicans and White House budget director Mick Mulvaney have predicted that they will have a CBO \u201cscore\u201d ready by early next week, before the bills are combined and brought before the House Budget Committee. GOP staffers noted that other health-care bills, including the 21st Century Cures Act and the 2015 reauthorization bill for Medicare and the Children\u2019s Health Insurance Program, began their journeys through congressional committees without a CBO score.\n\nDemocrats on the Energy and Commerce Committee moved to delay the bill\u2019s consideration for 30 days, while those on Ways and Means moved to delay it for one week to allow for further hearings and to examine the CBO report. Both motions were voted down on a straight party-line vote, and the panels continued working into the evening.\n\nStill, the most imminent threat GOP leaders must contend with comes from the far right. The speaker can lose only 21 Republican votes if the American Health Care Act is to pass, and opponents are promising to use that leverage to force changes to the bill.\n\nRep. Thomas Garrett (R-Va.), a freshman member of the conservative House Freedom Caucus, said he was confident that his camp could help reshape the legislation. While Trump had endorsed it already, Garrett said, he would probably be willing to accept something else if it were changed in a way conservatives could support.\n\n\u201cThis is a guy who said he wasn\u2019t sure that NATO, in its classic role, is necessary \u2014 and then two months later we saw countries like Germany vow to increase their defense spending to 2 percent of GDP,\u201d said Garrett. \u201cWhy\u2019d they do that? Because Donald Trump wasn\u2019t going to accept the status quo. They made a counteroffer. Right now, there\u2019s an offer, and he\u2019s saying he likes to get people to make counteroffers.\u201d\n\nVice President Pence met with two House Freedom Caucus leaders Tuesday, and that same day Mulvaney \u2014 a former caucus member \u2014 spent more than an hour at a meeting of the group. Its members have been invited to visit the White House next Tuesday.\n\nWednesday night, Trump met with leaders from Americans for Prosperity, the Club for Growth, FreedomWorks, the Heritage Foundation and its political arm, Heritage Action, and the Tea Party Patriots.\n\nBut the hard reality for Republicans is that any changes made to appease House conservatives could threaten the bill\u2019s support among moderates.\n\n\u201cMy fear is that the bill will go backwards,\u201d said Rep. Tom MacArthur (R-N.J.), a co-chairman of the centrist Tuesday Group who has gotten White House attention of his own: He met Tuesday with Health and Human Services Secretary Tom Price. \u201cIf the bill starts going in the reverse direction in order to satisfy certain members of my party, then I\u2019m going to have a problem.
