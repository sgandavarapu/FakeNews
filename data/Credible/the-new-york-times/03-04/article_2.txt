data-total-count=\"217\">SAN FRANCISCO \u2014 Uber has for years engaged in a worldwide program to deceive the authorities in markets where its low-cost ride-hailing service was resisted by law enforcement or, in some instances, had been banned.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"350\" data-total-count=\"567\">The program, involving a tool called Greyball, uses data collected from the Uber app and other techniques to identify and circumvent officials who were trying to clamp down on the ride-hailing service. Uber used these methods to evade the authorities in cities like <a href=\"http://bostinno.streetwise.co/2014/05/29/how-many-boston-uberx-drivers-have-been-ticketed-by-boston-police/\">Boston</a>, Paris and Las Vegas, and in countries like Australia, China and South Korea.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"350\" data-total-count=\"917\">Greyball was part of a program called VTOS, short for \u201cviolation of terms of service,\u201d which Uber created to root out people it thought were using or targeting its service improperly. The program, including Greyball, began as early as 2014 and remains in use, predominantly outside the United States. Greyball was approved by Uber\u2019s legal team.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"276\" data-total-count=\"1193\">Greyball and the VTOS program were described to The New York Times by four current and former Uber employees, who also provided documents. The four spoke on the condition of anonymity because the tools and their use are confidential and because of fear of retaliation by Uber.</p>        <a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-1\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n    </div><!-- close story-body -->\n    <div class=\"supplemental first\" id=\"supplemental-1\">\n    </div><!-- close supplemental -->\n</div><!-- close story-body-supplemental -->\n<div class=\"story-interrupter\" id=\"story-continues-1\">\n    <div id=\"FlexAd\" class=\"ad flex-ad nocontent robots-nocontent\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-2\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n    <div class=\"flex-ad-creative\"></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class=\"story-body-supplemental\">\n    <div class=\"story-body story-body-2\">\n        <p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"205\" data-total-count=\"1398\" id=\"story-continues-2\">Uber\u2019s use of Greyball was recorded on video in late 2014, when Erich England, a code enforcement inspector in Portland, Ore., tried to hail an Uber car downtown in a sting operation against the company.</p><div id=\"story-ad-1\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-3\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"362\" data-total-count=\"1760\" id=\"story-continues-3\">At the time, Uber had just started its ride-hailing service in Portland without seeking permission from the city, which later declared the service <a href=\"https://bits.blogs.nytimes.com/2014/12/08/portland-ore-sues-uber-for-operating-in-city/?_r=1\">illegal</a>. To build a case against the company, officers like Mr. England posed as riders, opening the Uber app to hail a car and watching as miniature vehicles on the screen made their way toward the potential fares.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"470\" data-total-count=\"2230\">But unknown to Mr. England and other authorities, some of the digital cars they saw in the app did not represent actual vehicles. And the Uber drivers they were able to hail also quickly canceled. That was because Uber had tagged Mr. England and his colleagues \u2014 essentially Greyballing them as city officials \u2014 based on data collected from the app and in other ways. The company then served up a fake version of the app, populated with ghost cars, to evade capture.</p><figure class=\"media video youtube embedded layout-large-horizontal\">\n        <iframe class=\"video-bind\" width=\"420\" height=\"315\" src=\"//youtube.com/embed/TS0NuV-zLZE?wmode=transparent\" allowfullscreen></iframe>\n    <figcaption class=\"caption\" itemprop=\"description\"><span class=\"caption-text\">Portland vs. Uber: City code officers try to ticket drivers</span> <span class=\"credit\" itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\">Video by The Oregonian</span></figcaption>\n</figure>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"411\" data-total-count=\"2641\">At a time when Uber is already under scrutiny for its <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2017/02/22/technology/uber-workplace-culture.html\">boundary-pushing workplace culture</a>, its use of the Greyball tool underscores the lengths to which the company will go to dominate its market. Uber has long flouted laws and regulations to gain an edge against entrenched transportation providers, a modus operandi that has helped propel it into more than 70 countries and to a valuation close to $70 billion.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-4\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-4\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"287\" data-total-count=\"2928\" id=\"story-continues-4\">Yet using its app to identify and sidestep the authorities where regulators said Uber was breaking the law goes further toward skirting ethical lines \u2014 and, potentially, legal ones. Some at Uber who knew of the VTOS program and how the Greyball tool was being used were troubled by it.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"312\" data-total-count=\"3240\">In a statement, Uber said, \u201cThis program denies ride requests to users who are violating our terms of service \u2014 whether that\u2019s people aiming to physically harm drivers, competitors looking to disrupt our operations, or opponents who collude with officials on secret \u2018stings\u2019 meant to entrap drivers.\u201d</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-6\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-5\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"171\" data-total-count=\"3411\" id=\"story-continues-5\">The mayor of Portland, Ted Wheeler, said in a statement, \u201cI am very concerned that Uber may have purposefully worked to thwart the city\u2019s job to protect the public.\u201d</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"305\" data-total-count=\"3716\">Uber, which lets people hail rides using a smartphone app, operates multiple types of services, including a luxury Black Car offering in which drivers are commercially licensed. But an Uber service that many regulators have had problems with is the lower-cost version, known in the United States as UberX.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-8\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-8 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-6\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"190\" data-total-count=\"3906\" id=\"story-continues-6\">UberX essentially lets people who have passed a background check and vehicle inspection become Uber drivers quickly. In the past, many cities have banned the service and declared it illegal.</p><div id=\"story-ad-2\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-7\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"336\" data-total-count=\"4242\" id=\"story-continues-7\">That is because the ability to summon a noncommercial driver \u2014 which is how UberX drivers using private vehicles are typically categorized \u2014 was often unregulated. In barreling into new markets, Uber capitalized on this lack of regulation to quickly enlist UberX drivers and put them to work before local regulators could stop them.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-10\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-10 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-8\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"346\" data-total-count=\"4588\" id=\"story-continues-8\">After the authorities caught on to what was happening, Uber and local officials often clashed. Uber has encountered legal problems over UberX in cities including Austin, Tex., Philadelphia and Tampa, Fla., as well as internationally. Eventually, agreements were reached under which regulators developed a legal framework for the low-cost service.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"312\" data-total-count=\"4900\">That approach has been costly. Law enforcement officials in some cities have impounded vehicles or issued tickets to UberX drivers, with Uber generally picking up those costs on the drivers\u2019 behalf. The company has estimated thousands of dollars in lost revenue for every vehicle impounded and ticket received.</p><figure id=\"media-100000004969196\" class=\"media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000004969196 ratio-tall\" data-media-action=\"modal\" itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemid=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/04/business/04-UBER-01/04-UBER-01-master675.jpg\" itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" aria-label=\"media\" role=\"group\">\n    <span class=\"visually-hidden\">Photo</span>\n    <div class=\"image\">\n            <img src=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/04/business/04-UBER-01/04-UBER-01-master675.jpg\" alt=\"\" class=\"media-viewer-candidate\" data-mediaviewer-src=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/04/business/04-UBER-01/04-UBER-01-superJumbo.jpg\" data-mediaviewer-caption=\"Uber&rsquo;s Greyball tool was developed to weed out riders thought to be using its service improperly.\" data-mediaviewer-credit=\"Mark Ralston/Agence France-Presse &mdash; Getty Images\"  itemprop=\"url\" itemid=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/04/business/04-UBER-01/04-UBER-01-master675.jpg\"/>\n            <meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"495\" />\n            <meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"675\" />\n    </div>\n        <figcaption class=\"caption\" itemprop=\"caption description\">\n                <span class=\"caption-text\">Uber\u2019s Greyball tool was developed to weed out riders thought to be using its service improperly.</span>\n                        <span class=\"credit\" itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\">\n            <span class=\"visually-hidden\">Credit</span>\n            Mark Ralston/Agence France-Presse \u2014 Getty Images        </span>\n            </figcaption>\n    </figure>\n<div id=\"story-ad--aggro-12\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-9\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"252\" data-total-count=\"5152\" id=\"story-continues-9\">This is where the VTOS program and the use of the Greyball tool came in. When Uber moved into a new city, it appointed a general manager to lead the charge. This person, using various technologies and techniques, would try to spot enforcement officers.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"366\" data-total-count=\"5518\">One technique involved drawing a digital perimeter, or \u201cgeofence,\u201d around the government offices on a digital map of a city that Uber was monitoring. The company watched which people were frequently opening and closing the app \u2014 a process known internally as eyeballing \u2014 near such locations as evidence that the users might be associated with city agencies.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"168\" data-total-count=\"5686\">Other techniques included looking at a user\u2019s credit card information and Occasionally, if a driver accidentally picked up someone tagged as an officer, Uber called the driver with instructions to end the ride.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-18\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-12\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"237\" data-total-count=\"7132\" id=\"story-continues-12\">Uber employees said the practices and tools were born in part out of safety measures meant to protect drivers in some countries. In France, India and Kenya, for instance, taxi companies and workers targeted and attacked new Uber drivers.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"321\" data-total-count=\"7453\">\u201cThey\u2019re beating the cars with metal bats,\u201d the singer Courtney Love posted on Twitter from an Uber car in Paris at a time of <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2015/06/26/business/international/uber-protests-france.html\">clashes between the company and taxi drivers</a> in 2015. Uber said that was still the tool\u2019s primary use.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"314\" data-total-count=\"8189\">But as Uber moved into new markets, its engineers saw that the same methods could be used to evade law enforcement. Once the Greyball tool was put in place and tested, Uber engineers created a playbook with a list of tactics and distributed it to general managers in more than a dozen countries on five continents.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"339\" data-total-count=\"8528\">At least 50 people inside Uber knew about Greyball, and some had qualms about whether it was ethical or legal. Greyball was approved by Uber\u2019s legal team, led by Salle Yoo, the company\u2019s general counsel. Ryan Graves, an early hire who became senior vice president of global operations and a board member, was also aware of the program.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-24\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-6 ad-aggro-8 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-14\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"63\" data-total-count=\"8591\" id=\"story-continues-14\">Ms. Yoo and Mr. Graves did not respond to requests for comment.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"364\" data-total-count=\"8955\">Outside legal specialists said they were uncertain about the legality of the program. Greyball could be considered a violation of the federal Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, or possibly intentional obstruction of justice, depending on local laws and jurisdictions, said Peter Henning, a law professor at Wayne State University who also writes for The New York Times.</p><div id=\"story-ad-4\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-15\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"295\" data-total-count=\"9250\" id=\"story-continues-15\">\u201cWith any type of systematic thwarting of the law, you\u2019re flirting with disaster,\u201d Professor Henning said. \u201cWe all take our foot off the gas when we see the police car at the intersection up ahead, and there\u2019s nothing wrong with that. But this goes far beyond avoiding a speed trap.\u201d</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"253\" data-total-count=\"9503\">On Friday, Marietje Schaake, a member of the European Parliament for the Dutch Democratic Party in the Netherlands, wrote that she had written <a href=\"https://marietjeschaake.eu/en/uber-s-use-of-greyball-tool\"> to the European Commission</a> asking, among other things, if it planned to investigate the legality of Greyball.</p><button class=\"button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large\" data-skip-to-para-id=\"\">\n</button>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"167\" data-total-count=\"9670\">To date, Greyballing has been effective.
