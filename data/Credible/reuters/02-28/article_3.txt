WASHINGTON</span> \n\t\t</div>\n<span id=\"midArticle_0\"></span><span class=\"article-prime\"><p><span class=\"articleLocation\">WASHINGTON</span> President Donald Trump told Congress on Tuesday he was open to immigration reform, shifting from his harsh rhetoric on illegal immigration in a speech that offered a more restrained tone than his election campaign and first month in the White House.</p></span><span id=\"midArticle_1\"></span><p>Trump, in a prime-time address to a country that remains divided over his leadership, set aside disputes with Democrats and the news media to deliver his most presidential performance to date, seeking to regain the confidence of Americans rattled by his leadership thus far.</p><span id=\"midArticle_2\"></span><p>The president's speech was long on promises but short on specifics on how to achieve a challenging legislative agenda that could add dramatically to budget deficits. He wants a healthcare overhaul, broad tax cuts and a $1 trillion public-private initiative to rebuild degraded roads and bridges.</p><span id=\"midArticle_3\"></span><p>Trump built a base of support behind his presidential campaign by vowing to fight illegal immigration. In his speech, he took a more moderate tone, appealing to Republicans and Democrats to work together on immigration reform.</p><span id=\"midArticle_4\"></span><p>He said it was possible if both Republicans and Democrats in Congress were willing to compromise, although he also said U.S. immigration should be based on a merit-based system, rather than relying on lower-skilled immigrants.</p><span id=\"midArticle_5\"></span><p>Comprehensive immigration reform eluded his two predecessors, Democrat Barack Obama and Republican George W. Bush, because of deep divisions within Congress and among Americans over the issue. Trump said reform would raise wages and help more struggling families enter the middle class.</p><span id=\"midArticle_6\"></span><p>\"I believe that real and positive immigration reform is possible, as long as we focus on the following goals: to improve jobs and wages for Americans, to strengthen our nation's security, and to restore respect for our laws,\" said the Republican president.</p><span id=\"midArticle_7\"></span><p>Trump's first month in office was dominated by a fight over his temporary travel ban on people from seven Muslim-majority nations, repeated attacks on the news media and harsh personal criticism of judges who blocked his immigration order.</p><span id=\"midArticle_8\"></span><p>In his speech on Tuesday, he appeared to look for a reset, trying to move past a chaotic period that sowed doubts about his ability to govern effectively.</p><span id=\"midArticle_9\"></span><p>\"The time for trivial fights is behind us,\" he said.</p><span id=\"midArticle_10\"></span><p></p><span class=\"article-subtitle\"><p>PARTISAN DIVIDE</p>\n</span><span id=\"midArticle_11\"></span>\n                \n                <span class=\"article-divide first-article-divide\"></span><p>Financial markets showed a muted reaction as Trump gave few specifics or new details.</p><span id=\"midArticle_12\"></span><p>Early signs showed Trump's speech drew a positive response. A CNN/ORC poll found 57 percent of viewers thought Trump's speech was very positive and 69 percent said they felt more optimistic about the future of the country.</p><span id=\"midArticle_13\"></span><p>\"He's lost his foot and mouth disease,\" said Tom Beckwith, 71, of Seminole, Florida. \"This was a tremendous speech. He's cured.\"</p><span id=\"midArticle_14\"></span><p>Still, there was plenty of evidence of ongoing partisan divisions. In the chamber of the House of Representatives where Trump spoke, Democrats sometimes sat in silence and turned their thumbs down at his remarks. Many women lawmakers wore white in a subtle show of protest.</p><span id=\"midArticle_15\"></span><p>Democratic Senator Christopher Coons of Delaware called Trump's speech one of \"the most coherent public addresses he's given in a month.\"</p><span id=\"midArticle_16\"></span><p>But Coons said he viewed Trump\u2019s forthcoming budget proposals as \u201cgravely concerning,\" citing the president's plan for paying for a defense buildup by cutting foreign aid and other programs.</p><span id=\"midArticle_17\"></span>\n                \n                <span class=\"article-divide second-article-divide\"></span><p>Much of Trump's speech focused on solving problems at home in line with his \"America first\" rhetoric. Apart from criticizing Obama for increasing the national debt, Trump did not mention the federal budget deficit, which will severely limit any new spending programs.</p><span id=\"midArticle_18\"></span><p>On immigration, Trump again pledged to build a wall on the U.S.-Mexico border, but he made no mention this time of Mexico paying for it. He also said he would \u201cshortly take new steps to keep our nation safe,\u201d referring to a new executive order he is to sign to replace one embroiled in the courts.</p><span id=\"midArticle_19\"></span><p>Trump focused part of the speech on foreign policy, stressing his support for NATO but insisting allies pay more for their defense.</p><span id=\"midArticle_20\"></span><p>In a possible nod to his bid to warm relations with Russia, which he did not mention by name, Trump said: \"America is willing to find new friends, and to forge new partnerships, where shared interests align.\"</p><span id=\"midArticle_21\"></span><p>Trump has been criticized for his praise of Russian President Vladimir Putin. A U.S. congressional committee is investigating contacts between Trump's election campaign and Russia to see if there were any inappropriate communications.</p><span id=\"midArticle_22\"></span><p>\"We want harmony and stability, not war and conflict,\" said Trump, who said, however, he would embark on a big defense buildup.</p><span id=\"midArticle_23\"></span>\n                \n                <span class=\"article-divide third-article-divide\"></span><p></p><span class=\"article-subtitle\"><p>'MASSIVE TAX RELIEF'</p>\n</span><span id=\"midArticle_24\"></span><p>Trump said he wanted to provide \"massive tax relief\" to the middle class and cut corporate tax rates. But he did not offer specifics and failed to comment on the most pressing tax issue facing Congress, a proposed border adjustment tax to boost exports over imports.</p><span id=\"midArticle_25\"></span><p>Lawmakers have been looking to Trump for more leadership on an issue that has divided corporate America and Republicans in Congress.</p><span id=\"midArticle_26\"></span><p>Trump called on the Republican-led Congress to repeal and replace Obamacare with reforms that expand choice, increase access and lower costs. </p><span id=\"midArticle_27\"></span><p>Republicans remain divided on how to accomplish that goal and Democrats vehemently oppose tampering with a system that provides health insurance for millions of low-income Americans.</p><span id=\"midArticle_28\"></span><p>While Trump's comments lacked detail, it was the first time he publicly supported tax credits to help Americans purchase their own coverage, a nod to health insurers who say they are necessary to keep people in the market.</p><span id=\"midArticle_29\"></span><p>Former Kentucky Governor Steve Beshear said in the Democratic response to Trump's speech that \"you and your Republican allies in Congress seem determined to rip affordable health insurance away from millions of Americans who most need it.\"</p><span id=\"midArticle_30\"></span><p>In the most emotional moment of the night, Trump singled out Carryn Owens, the widow of U.S Navy SEAL William \"Ryan\" Owens, who was killed in a raid on al Qaeda in Yemen. </p><span id=\"midArticle_31\"></span><p>Owens, tears streaming down her face, clasped her hands and looked upward from her spot in the balcony as lawmakers and the president applauded her in the longest ovation of Trump's hour-long speech. </p><span id=\"midArticle_32\"></span><p>Trump said the mission that her husband participated in obtained vital intelligence that could be used against Islamist militants, taking issue with news reports quoting U.S. officials who said little was gained from the raid.</p><span id=\"midArticle_33\"></span><p></p><p></p><p> (Additional reporting by But he did not offer specifics and failed to comment on the most pressing tax issue facing Congress, a proposed border adjustment tax to boost exports over imports.\n\nLawmakers have been looking to Trump for more leadership on an issue that has divided corporate America and Republicans in Congress.\n\nTrump called on the Republican-led Congress to repeal and replace Obamacare with reforms that expand choice, increase access and lower costs.\n\nRepublicans remain divided on how to accomplish that goal and Democrats vehemently oppose tampering with a system that provides health insurance for millions of low-income Americans.\n\nWhile Trump's comments lacked detail, it was the first time he publicly supported tax credits to help Americans purchase their own coverage, a nod to health insurers who say they are necessary to keep people in the market.\n\nFormer Kentucky Governor Steve Beshear said in the Democratic response to Trump's speech that \"you and your Republican allies in Congress seem determined to rip affordable health insurance away from millions of Americans who most need it.\"\n\nIn the most emotional moment of the night, Trump singled out Carryn Owens, the widow of U.S Navy SEAL William \"Ryan\" Owens, who was killed in a raid on al Qaeda in Yemen.\n\nOwens, tears streaming down her face, clasped her hands and looked upward from her spot in the balcony as lawmakers and the president applauded her in the longest ovation of Trump's hour-long speech.\n\nTrump said the mission that her husband participated in obtained vital intelligence that could be used against Islamist militants, taking issue with news reports quoting U.S. officials who said little was gained from the raid.\n\n(Additional reporting by Richard Cowan, Ayesha Rascoe, Jeff Mason, Emily Stephenson, Doina Chiacu in Washington, Megan Davies in New York and Letitia Stein in Clearwater, Fla.;
